What actually predicts what, in analytics engineering job descriptions

Single percentages ("82% rigour-oriented") are cheap and mostly uninformative on their own — every JD in a rigour-dominant market says similar things. What's useful is where two dimensions move together: does seniority predict autonomy? Does team maturity predict what kind of work you'll do? This page leads with those relationships, shows the test statistic behind each one (recomputed live against whatever filter is active), and reports the relationships that failed — including one this page used to claim that dissolved when the corpus doubled.

Read before trusting any number here: this is a pilot-scale, single-market dataset (Europe-heavy, collected opportunistically during one person's job search). Confidence intervals are wide — treat percentages as directional, not precise. Geography reflects what got scraped, not the market; see the callout in that section.

Two-variable findings

Where dimensions move together

Each panel below crosses two classifications to answer a question a single-dimension stat can't: not "how many roles are strategic" but "which roles are strategic." Panels are ordered by effect size (Cramér's V), strongest first, and each carries its χ² test — recomputed against the active role filter. Coverage, not a finding

Where these JDs came from — and why that's not a market map

This is a corpus-coverage statement, not a geographic finding

The regional split below reflects what got scraped, not where demand exists. It's a keyword match on free-text location strings collected opportunistically during one person's job search — sourced from whatever job boards and company pages surfaced roles worth applying to. A city appears here because a posting was found and classified, not because analytics engineering hiring is concentrated there.

Roughly a quarter of records fall into "other" — locations too ambiguous to bucket (multi-city remote listings, unstated locations, or cities outside the keyword list). Treat every number in this section as a description of the dataset's blind spots, not the labour market's shape.

A concrete example of the artifact: "Global remote" postings hard-gate on language (typically German) more often than Berlin postings do in this bucketing — which is backwards from what you'd expect if the categories meant what they sound like. The likely cause: a remote role that requires German gets classified as "global remote" by the location-string matcher rather than folded into the Berlin/DACH bucket, because the matcher keys off literal city names, not the language requirement itself. Read that as a bug in the bucketing, not a claim that remote roles gate on language more than local ones.
